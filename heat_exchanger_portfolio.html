Project Overview

Chill a water bath at 0.3 °C/min

The goal was to size a double-pipe heat exchanger to cool a stationary water volume at a controlled rate of 0.3 °C/min using R-134a as the coolant. Water flows through the inner copper tube; refrigerant flows in the annular gap. Wall conduction resistance is negligible relative to the convective resistances on both sides.

Water-Side Convection via Gnielinski

With ReD = 2445, the flow sits in the transitional-to-turbulent regime. The Gnielinski correlation was applied using the Moody friction factor from the explicit Colebrook approximation (Haaland form):

Design Point Selection

Operating Point & Design Iteration

Off-design curves swept coolant temperature and flow rate to map the feasibility boundary. The minimum required flow rate rises sharply as coolant temperature approaches the process water temperature (~20 °C), creating a near-vertical asymptote. Two candidate points were initially evaluated — both near that asymptote:

Point Tcoolant,in V̇ (min) Q̇ Assessment
A — original 19.1 °C 9.88 L/min 17.74 W Asymptote — 0% margin
B — 15% buffer 18.9 °C 14.34 L/min 20.40 W Asymptote — margin only
Revised — elbow ~12.5–17.5 °C ~1–2 L/min >17.74 W Elbow Region — robust

Design Flaw — Both A and B Sit on the Asymptote

Point A was selected under the reasoning that a warmer coolant temperature minimizes refrigeration work. Point B then added a 15% flow rate buffer on top of A. However, both points remain on the near-vertical portion of the design curve — the asymptote where a tiny drop in coolant temperature demands a massive, potentially unachievable surge in flow rate. Adding margin in flow rate does not escape the asymptotic regime; it only delays the problem slightly. The system at either point would be extremely difficult to control in practice.

Corrected Approach — Select from the Elbow Region

The robust operating region lies along the gradual bend of the design curve, roughly between 12.5 °C and 17.5 °C coolant inlet temperature with flow rates in the 1–2 L/min range. Any point along this stretch of the curve is a valid, defensible design choice. In this region, the curve is relatively flat: large changes in coolant temperature require only modest changes in flow rate, giving the control system adequate authority and providing natural margin against disturbances. This is the standard industry approach — trade a small increase in refrigeration duty for a dramatically more controllable and robust operating point.

The four-panel analysis below shows the full off-design picture: top-left confirms the steep asymptote near 19–20 °C; top-right shows diminishing returns in Q̇ as flow rate increases beyond ~5 L/min; bottom-left shows that at the design flow rate the system loses margin rapidly as coolant temperature climbs; bottom-right confirms the HX is sufficient up to ~0.29 °C/min process heating at fixed coolant conditions.

This project demonstrated that optimal thermal design is not purely about maximizing thermodynamic efficiency at a single point. Operating near a curve asymptote — even with a flow rate buffer — creates a system that is impractical to control. The corrected approach selects from the elbow region of the design curve, where the tradeoff between refrigeration duty and controllability is well-balanced and the system behaves robustly under real-world disturbances.
